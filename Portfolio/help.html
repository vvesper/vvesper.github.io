Ways to Support Bee Populations


About 40% of honey comes from small-time beekeepers. Taking care of a colony is a part-time job that bee lovers and hobbyists alike can undertake by preparing themselves with man resources. Even if you're not a beekeeper yourself, there are some ways you can better support bees in your backyard or garden. Though many people are afraid of bees, these amazing pollinators need to be protected for the future.

Plant Seeds

There are flowers you can plant that are more hospitable to bees and other pollinators. Other endangered insects such as Monarch butterflies benefit from these plants as well. Check out the Pollinator database for plants and flowers that bees can pollinate and are indigenous to your area. One thing that you may not know is that bees get thirsty! Leave out a shallow dish of water for your bees to hydrate themselves.

Buy Local

If you can't become a backyard beekeeper, buy honey from your local farmer's market or nearby farms. This serves to support individuals and organizations who are taking measures to support hives and colonies. Buying honey from local farmers and apiarists is more sustainable than purchasing it from the store. Supporting local beekeepers means you are supporting hives in your area that are thriving off of indigenous flora and are well-maintained.

Spread Awareness

Educate yourself and others about resources in your area and nationwide that can help the effort towards preserving bee populations. Consider donating to ecological non-profits such as or other organizations that are committed to such as the Honeybee Conservancy. If you do not have the time or resources to devote to maintain your own backyard colony, The Honeybee Conservancy has a program called Sponsor-a-hive which partners with educational organizations and local food initiatives to raise children with a knowledge of bees and combat food deserts.
